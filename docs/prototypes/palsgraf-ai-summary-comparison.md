## Citation-Aware Comparison Brief

### Facts

Palsgraf was waiting on the defendant railroad's platform when two men ran to board a moving train. Railroad guards helped one man aboard, dislodging a newspaper-wrapped package from his hands. The package contained concealed fireworks. Its explosion caused scales located many feet away to fall and injure Palsgraf. [Opinion `op-005`, `op-009`]

### Issue

Did the railroad owe Palsgraf a duty of care when the package appeared harmless and her injury was outside the range of danger the guards could reasonably perceive? [Opinion `op-127`, `op-161`]

### Holding

No. The railroad's conduct was not negligent in relation to Palsgraf because the guards could not reasonably foresee that handling the apparently harmless package would endanger her. The court reversed the judgments below and dismissed her complaint. [Opinion `op-013`, `op-247`]

### Rule

A negligence plaintiff must establish a duty owed to that plaintiff. The reasonably perceptible risk defines both the duty and the persons to whom it is owed; negligence toward one person does not by itself create liability to an unforeseeable plaintiff. [Opinion `op-127`, `op-161`, `op-209`]

### Majority Reasoning

Cardozo treated duty as relational. Palsgraf could recover only for invasion of her own legally protected interest, not as the indirect beneficiary of a possible wrong to the package holder. Because nothing about the package warned of danger to a distant passenger, the railroad committed no wrong against Palsgraf. Without a duty and corresponding wrong, proximate cause never became relevant. [Opinion `op-013`, `op-127`, `op-161`, `op-209`]

### Dissent

Andrews argued that everyone owes society a general duty to avoid conduct that unreasonably threatens others. Once an act is negligent, liability should turn on whether the injury is a proximate consequence, not whether the particular plaintiff was within a foreseeable zone of danger. He would have allowed Palsgraf to recover because the explosion was a substantial factor in a direct and continuous sequence, and injury to someone on the platform was a probable result of the explosion. [Opinion `op-257`, `op-313`, `op-499`, `op-505`]

### Significance

The case frames the enduring disagreement between a plaintiff-specific duty analysis and a broader duty followed by limits based on proximate cause. This statement is editorial synthesis and is intentionally not attributed to the opinion itself.

## Observations

The existing Claude brief is substantively good. It captures Cardozo's relational-duty theory, Andrews's broader duty theory, the procedural result, and the case's pedagogical importance. Its main weakness is not summary quality; it is that readers cannot immediately tell which statements come directly from the opinion and which are later doctrinal synthesis.

The citation-aware brief is not dramatically "smarter." Its advantage is provenance. It can take a student directly from a rule, holding, or reasoning statement to the language supporting it. It is also more explicit when a section, such as Significance, is not based solely on the source opinion.

Preserve the current brief style, but add structured source fields for Facts, Issue, Holding, Rule, Majority Reasoning, and Dissent. Keep Significance as a separately labeled editorial section. This combines the current Claude brief's readability with the citation-aware version's verifiability.
